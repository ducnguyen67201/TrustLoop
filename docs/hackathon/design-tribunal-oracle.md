## Problem Statement

Every LLM on earth ships confidently wrong answers. There is no economic layer that prices truth at sub-cent granularity, in real time, across heterogeneous evidence sources. Agents have no trustworthy way to verify each other's outputs, so the "agentic economy" keeps running on vibes.

Circle's Nanopayments + Arc unlock a primitive that was never economically viable before: **sub-cent per-claim markets with recursive sub-markets**. That is the exact shape of Paul Christiano's AI-safety-via-debate proposal — a 2018 research idea that needed 2026's settlement rail to actually run.

**Tribunal** is the first implementation. It is the truth oracle layer for the agentic economy.

---

## What Makes This Cool

- **The first live implementation of debate-based scalable oversight of AI.** ML/safety-savvy judges will recognize this on sight.
- **Recursive debate trees with nanopayment cascades.** Claims decompose into sub-claims, each with its own binary market. Bets settle upward. Tx count per query: 1,500–5,000 nanopayments.
- **Budget-bounded, not depth-clamped.** A user's USDC budget governs how hard the swarm thinks. Cheap claims resolve in a shallow tree; controversial claims spawn deep ones. The price IS the difficulty signal.
- **MCP-native surface.** Every Claude Desktop, Cursor, Cline, Windsurf, MCP-aware agent in the world can call `verify(claim, budget)` out of the box. Zero-friction distribution.
- **Agent reputation emerges from prices.** Auditor agents don't have star ratings. They have tradeable reputation prices that move with every market they resolve.

## Premises

1. **Budget-bounded > depth-clamped.** The tree grows as deep as the budget allows. No arbitrary depth limits. No arbitrary oracle-type limits. The price of a claim reflects the depth of debate needed to resolve it. *(User explicitly rejected artificial clamping — this is load-bearing.)*
2. **MCP is the right surface.** Any AI agent on earth can call Tribunal via MCP with zero setup. Free distribution, zero-friction demo on stage.
3. **Leaf judges are LLM+oracle-tool, not consensus.** Each terminal sub-claim is resolved by a small LLM with a single oracle call (SEC, Wikipedia, Wolfram, GitHub, PubMed, Chainlink price feeds, datetime, etc.). Debate/markets happen at the speculation layer ABOVE leaves, not at the truth layer. This is boring but bulletproof.
4. **Tx volume scales naturally.** Math: 1 root claim → ~40 sub-claims avg → ~15 specialists staking per market → ~1,200 stakes + ~300 cascade payouts = 1,500+ nanopayments per single user verification. Beats Circle's implicit "nanopayments actually nano" gate by 30x.
5. **The demo narrative sells the product.** "Claude Desktop asked Tribunal 'Did Tesla IPO in 2008?' and it spawned 47 sub-debates, spent 1.9¢, and returned a verdict with full evidence chain in 12 seconds." That sentence is the whole pitch.

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: Budget-bounded tree (CHOSEN)

Single USDC budget at root. Tree grows dynamically. When budget near-zero, expansion stops and partial-resolution prices stand. Ship: MCP server + recursive orchestrator + 10 oracle leaf-types + live tree viz + agent reputation registry on Arc.

- **Pros:** Ambitious enough to be novel, scoped enough to ship. Dynamic depth shows off Arc economics organically. Leaves judges with a story, not a toy.
- **Cons:** Orchestrator logic is the technical crux. If recursion misfires, demo fails.
- **Effort:** M (6 days, realistic).
- **Risk:** Medium.

### Approach B: Two-layer economy (max ambition)

A + meta-market on specialist reputation + ERC-8004 identity. Specialists have tradeable rep tokens; traders short bad auditors. Two live orderbooks on screen.

- **Pros:** Unprecedented. Nobody else ships this. Demo is a financial instrument.
- **Cons:** 6-day completion probability ~55%. A partial ship looks worse than a polished A.
- **Effort:** L.
- **Risk:** High.

### Approach C: Voice-first oracle (the Hamming edge)

A + live phone call demo: caller speaks a claim, voice agent orchestrates the debate, narrates the tree resolving in real time.

- **Pros:** Unfair edge nobody matches. Audio spectacle on stage.
- **Cons:** Voice on venue wifi = risk. Judges may see it as a gimmick layered on the real product.
- **Effort:** M-L.
- **Risk:** Medium-high.

## Recommended Approach: A (Budget-bounded tree)

## Open Questions

1. **Claim decomposition prompt.** How does the orchestrator decide which sub-claims to spawn? Static template vs. meta-LLM planner? (Recommend: meta-LLM planner with a strict output schema — low risk, high flexibility.)
2. **Market mechanism.** Constant-product AMM (Uniswap-style) or LMSR (standard for prediction markets)? LMSR is more correct for probabilities; AMM is simpler to code. Recommend LMSR if 1-day affordable, else AMM.
3. **Specialist agent identity.** Are specialists a fixed roster (ship with 20 pre-seeded agents) or spawned dynamically per claim type? Recommend: fixed roster for demo, dynamic registration via MCP in v2.
4. **How do specialists form opinions?** Each specialist is an LLM persona with a biased prompt (e.g., "Skeptic: always look for falsification"). They see the claim + budget + current market price and decide to stake. Needs tuning.
5. **Failure modes in live demo.** What if the judge supplies a claim that can't decompose? Graceful fallback: single-level market with LLM-judge resolution + refund.
